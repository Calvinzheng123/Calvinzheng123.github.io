Project 3: Design & Storytelling — Amazon Product Engagement

Viz Analytics + Data Mining — Design, Storytelling, and Communication Mastery

Overview

This project is a design-focused evolution of my Amazon Product Engagement dashboard. The goal was not just to build a functional dashboard, but to create a portfolio-ready story with intentional color, layout, audience targeting, and narrative flow.

Launch Live Dashboard → Back to Portfolio →
  • Amazon-themed dark dashboard with filters for category, price band, coupon, and sponsorship.
  • Visuals focused on rating distributions and purchase vs rating patterns to separate visibility vs satisfaction problems.
  • What-if prediction tool that estimates total reviews based on rating, pricing, and promo levers.

Audience & Intent

The dashboard is designed for Amazon category and merchandising managers deciding where to invest in coupons, discounts, and sponsored placements. The core question is:

“For a given slice of the catalog, are we facing a visibility problem or a satisfaction problem?”

I want the audience to:

  • Quickly understand rating quality and engagement (total reviews) for a chosen slice.
  • See how sponsored placements and coupons show up in purchase behavior.
  • Run simple what-if scenarios around rating, pricing, and promotions.
  • Walk away with a mental model: high rating + low reviews = under-exposed, low rating + purchases = risk.

Story & Structure

The dashboard is structured as a linear story so users don’t have to guess where to look:

  1. Hero + KPIs: A dark Amazon-style header and four metrics (average rating, total reviews, average discount, best-seller share) give a quick read on overall health for the filtered slice.
  2. Rating distribution: A histogram shows whether this slice is mostly high quality or noisy. This builds trust in the slice before introducing promotions.
  3. Purchases vs rating (sponsored vs organic): A scatter plot of purchased_last_month vs product_rating, colored by placement (Sponsored ad vs Organic result), reveals how promos and exposure shift behavior.
  4. What-if prediction tool: A small panel lets the user plug in rating, price, discount, coupon, sponsorship, and category, then returns a predicted number of total reviews. This moves the story from descriptive to prescriptive.
  5. Model diagnostics (log–log view): An expandable section shows actual vs predicted reviews in log–log space, with R² and MAE. This gives technically-minded users a sense of reliability without cluttering the main story.

Overall, the flow is: overview → quality → behavior → action → reliability, which matches how a manager would reason through decisions.

Design Choices

Color & Theme

I used an Amazon-inspired dark theme: navy backgrounds (#131921 / #232F3E), orange accents (#FF9900), and light text. This keeps the dashboard visually cohesive and aligns it with a recognizable brand.

Plotly is configured with a dark template so axes, labels, and gridlines remain readable on the dark background. Sponsored vs organic products use distinct categorical colors; coupon status is shown in hover tooltips instead of adding more legend entries.

Layout & Hierarchy

The dashboard uses a single, vertical column with clear section headers (1–4). KPIs sit at the top as an “at-a-glance” summary. Each subsequent section answers a more detailed question: Is this slice trustworthy?, How do promos show up?, What happens if I change levers?, Can I trust the model?

Short captions under each chart explain how a manager should use it (e.g., “High rating, low purchases: good candidates for coupons or sponsorship.”). This guides attention without forcing the user to read a long report.

Clutter Reduction

I intentionally limited the main visuals to: a histogram, a scatter, a small what-if form + metric, and one diagnostic chart inside an expander. Extra detail (product title, total reviews, discount percent, coupon label) lives in hover tooltips.

Categories are capped to the top 30 with an “Other” bucket so the filters and legends stay readable and don’t turn into a wall of labels.

Perception & Accessibility

Quantitative variables use position (x/y) rather than color intensity, which matches how humans perceive differences best. Color is reserved for categorical distinctions (placement) and occasional highlighting.

The dark theme uses high-contrast text for readability, and the scatter plots use SVG rendering instead of WebGL to avoid browser-specific rendering issues and warnings.

Reflection

What worked well: tying each visual to a concrete decision (protect top performers, promote high-rated low-visibility items, and sanity-check promo ideas) made the dashboard feel practical instead of academic. The Amazon-style dark theme helped it look closer to a real internal tool.

What was challenging: the hardest part was balancing interactivity and focus. It was tempting to add time-series charts, product-level drilldowns, and more filters, but that would have diluted the narrative and made the dashboard feel like a control panel instead of a story.

What I would refine with more time: I would add time-based views around promo changes (before/after sponsorship or coupons), segment products by lifecycle (new launches vs long-tail items), and expose simple scenario presets in the prediction tool (for example, “new launch with intro coupon” vs “mature product with no promo”) to make the what-if workflow faster.

Access & Transparency

The live dashboard is hosted on Streamlit and linked at the top of this page. The underlying code and data pipelines are available in my project repository (and can be shared on request).

I used ChatGPT to help debug deployment issues (model loading, theming) and to iterate on the wording of the design report. The modeling choices, feature engineering, and final visual structure are my own; AI was used as a support tool, not as the source of the analysis.
