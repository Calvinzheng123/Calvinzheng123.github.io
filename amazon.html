Project 1: Visual Analytics - Amazon Product Engagement

Dataset: ikramshah512 — Amazon Products Sales (≈42k items, 2025).

Story: What drives engagement?

I examined how discounts, ratings, and category differences relate to engagement (proxied by total_reviews). The narrative uses 5 visuals and short takeaways which you can read below.

Problem

I wanted to explore what the drivers of product engagement on Amazon were : (1) how reviews are distributed, (2) whether ratings separate winners from average, (3) which categories dominate, and (4) how discounting affects reviews and satisfaction.

Data (for readers)

CSV with columns: discounted_price, original_price, discount_percentage, product_rating, total_reviews, product_category. ~42k scraped products. Reviews/ratings are proxies for engagement, not sales.

Visualizations

What I Learned

  • Engagement is highly unequal meaning a few products dominate reviews while others arent close.
  • Ratings are compressed which is shown by how volume signals visibility more than quality.
  • Category visibility matters more than rating differences.
  • 20–50% discounts align with more reviews; 50%+ doesn’t move ratings which could mean people are used to the discounts.

Impact & Limits

Findings suggest discount strategy can raise visibility (reviews), but doesn’t boost satisfaction (ratings). Snapshot data, no time dimension; reviews ≠ revenue; discount parsing may be noisy. Not causal.

Dataset origin & description

Source: Kaggle — Amazon Products Sales (≈42k items, 2025). Columns used: discounted_price, original_price, discount_percentage, product_rating, total_reviews, product_category. One row per product; no timestamps provided (snapshot). Credibility: scraped public listings; acceptable for visual analytics practice, not official Amazon data.

Preprocessing & cleaning (with rationale)

  • Type coercion: cast prices/ratings/reviews to numeric; invalid strings → NaN.
  • Missing handling: drop rows missing key fields (product_rating, total_reviews, discounted_price).
  • Outliers: clip extreme total_reviews at a high percentile for plots; use log scale to show long-tail without distorting the data too much.
  • Discount bands: created categorical discount_band (0%, 1–19%, 20–49%, 50%+) to reduce noise in discount analysis.
  • Category focus: restrict to top N categories by count when comparing distributions to avoid chart clutter.

Visualization & design choices

  • Review distribution (hist/log): long-tail, log x-axis communicates inequality without hiding small counts of data (before was in millions).
  • Ratings distribution: simple histogram shows compression at 4–5; violin/box optional for variance by category.
  • Category comparisons: bar/box with ordered categories by mean reviews and kept labels short to avoid wrapping.
  • Discount vs reviews: group by discount_band to reduce noise.
  • Discount vs ratings: flat trend warns against assuming discounts improve satisfaction probably due to similarity in a lot of the data.

Reflection

  • What worked: log-scale review distribution and discount bands made the story better and not as cluttered.
  • What didn’t: ratings lacked variance unfortunately and resulted in the scatterplots being noisy without binning.
  • Next time: add a time dimension if I can find it somewhere, and include sales/units to move beyond basic metrics.
